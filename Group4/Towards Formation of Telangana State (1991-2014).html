1. Early Advocacy: 1985–1988

1987 – Early Advocacy

E.V. Padmanabham, a Telangana supporter and former councillor, used his newspaper Flash & Fellowman (printed at T. Prabhakar’s Natyakala Press) to highlight injustices against Telangana. Frequent discussions between Padmanabham and Prabhakar laid the groundwork for organised documentation.

Telangana Information Centre

Inspired by Padmanabham, intellectuals including Prof. Jayashankar, Prof. Keshav Rao Jadhav, and T. Prabhakar formed the Telangana Information Centre. It collected historical evidence of discrimination from sources such as Osmania University Library.

Key Contributors:

  • T. Prabhakar
  • Prof. Lakshman (Philosophy Dept., OU)
  • Harinath

Publications

  • Reprints from the 1969 Telangana Movement
  • Monthly magazines edited by Prof. Ram Reddy
  • S.R.C. Report
  • Gentlemen’s Agreement
  • All-Party Agreement
  • G.O. 36
  • Four-page pamphlet on Fazal Ali Commission warnings

Telangana Information Trust (14 July 1988)

The Trust was established to expand this work, with its headquarters at T. Prabhakar’s residence in Khairtabad.

Twin Goals:

  • Research & Documentation – publishing reports on regional injustices.
  • Awareness & Advocacy – spreading knowledge across Telangana.

2. Ma Telangana Newspaper and Key Publications

Launch (13 August 1989)

The Telangana Information Trust launched the Ma Telangana newspaper at Basant Talkies, Kachiguda. The event was chaired by Keshav Rao Jadhav; Justice Konda Madhav Reddy was the chief guest.

Prominent Attendees:

  • Kaloji Narayana Rao
  • Prof. Jayashankar
  • Prof. Anand Rao Thota
  • Prof. Madhusudhan Reddy
  • P. Yashoda Devi

Book – Perspectives on Telangana

The Trust published this book documenting exploitation in the electricity and irrigation sectors. It played a crucial role in highlighting economic and political injustices.

Special Editions

  • 1989 – Covering the Kalvakurthi elections (where N.T. Rama Rao contested and lost).
  • 1997 – Coinciding with the revival of the Telangana movement.
  • 2001 – Marking the formation of Telangana Rashtra Samithi (TRS).

These editions helped shape public opinion and strengthened the demand for a separate Telangana state.

3. Impact on Student Activism (1990–91)

The literature published by the Trust and Ma Telangana inspired students at Osmania University to form organisations dedicated to the Telangana cause. Two major student groups emerged:

  • Telangana Students Front
  • Telangana Liberation Students Organization (TLSO)

These groups began mobilising students, setting the stage for more intensive agitation in the following years.

4. Telangana Mukthi Morcha (TMM) – 1993

Formation

TMM was established in 1993 by activists including N. Kishan Rao (convenor, former minister), K. Purushotham Reddy, Ch. Laxmaiah (ex MLA), S. Nagasena Reddy (advocate), Madan Mohan, Madhav Reddy, Srinivas, and Qadir.

Ideology

Besides advocating a separate Telangana, TMM called for the division of larger Indian states (U.P., M.P., Maharashtra, Bihar, etc.) into smaller ones, citing the progress of smaller states like Haryana, Kerala, Goa, and Punjab.

Activities

TMM conducted political training sessions (24–28 September 1993) arguing that smaller states ensure stable governance, better employment, drinking water, irrigation, and medical facilities.

Its resolutions gained support from figures like Justice T.L.N. Reddy. The Morcha maintained that Telangana had seen negligible development since its merger and that a separate state was essential for comprehensive development.

5. Telangana Liberation Students Organization (TLSO)

Background: Seat Allocation Grievance

Under the Six Point Formula, 85% (later 80%) of college seats were reserved for local Telangana students, while the remaining were open to both local and non-local students through open competition. Telangana students alleged that the open competition seats were disproportionately awarded to Seemandhra students.

1992 – Student Agitation Begins

Dissatisfied with this practice, students led by Manohar Reddy demanded that open competition seats also be allocated to local candidates. This marked the start of a non-political student agitation.

Formation of TLSO

To intensify the movement, TLSO was formally established under the guidance of Manohar Reddy, Krishna Mohan, Jagan Reddy, and other student leaders. It organised rallies, dharnas, conferences, group meetings, and strikes, primarily centred on Osmania University.

Key Events

  • 1 November 1992 – Protest meetings and rallies against AP Formation Day.
  • Two-day Telangana Saddassu – Leaders like Surendar Mohan and George Fernandez participated.
  • Deputation Controversy – GO sent 71 Seemandhra lecturers on deputation to Telangana.
  • Hunger Strike – Manohar Reddy began an indefinite hunger strike at Osmania University.

After three days, the Vijay Bhaskar Reddy government rescinded the GO.

Broader Impact

TLSO’s focus expanded from student issues to wider injustices in Telangana, making it a significant voice in the larger movement for regional rights and statehood.

  • These were formally inaugurated later on 19 January 1997 at Ashok Talkies, Hyderabad.

Decision taken to hold a Telangana Conference in Bhuvanagiri on 8 March 1997.

4. State Assembly Discussion (26 February 1997)

T. Jeevan Reddy (Congress, Jagityal) presented detailed statistics on the injustice and exploitation of Telangana, bringing the issue to the centre of state politics.

5. Bhuvanagiri Sabha (8–9 March 1997): A Watershed Moment

Origins

Nagaram Anjaiah, after parting ways with Manda Krishna Madiga (MRPS), formed the Telangana Madiga Reservation Porata Samithi. He collaborated with Gade Innaiah and Gadar to organise a large-scale meeting.

Planning

The idea emerged from the 19 January 1997 conference at Ashok Talkies; detailed planning took place at Ravindra Bharati’s Mini Hall.

Venue

Indian Mission High School, Bhuvanagiri; titled “Dagapadda Telangana”. The ground was renamed Nizam Vythireka Porata Amaraveerula Pranganam.

Attendance

Inaugurated by Kaloji Narayana Rao, with an estimated 40,000 people – intellectuals, lawyers, artists, and the general public.

Significance

Gadar’s performance of “Naa Thalli Telangana Thiraga Badda Veena” stirred deep emotions. The Sabha marked the transformation of the movement into a broad-based people’s uprising.

6. Telangana Kala Samithi (1998)

Affiliation

Affiliated with Telangana Jana Sabha; aimed to promote Telangana’s cultural heritage and mobilise people through art.

Leadership

  • Jahangir – Convenor
  • Belli Lalitha – Co-Convenor (active in Nalgonda)

Belli Lalitha became a powerful voice through her songs. She was brutally murdered on 26 May 1999 in Bhuvanagiri, highlighting the risks faced by cultural activists in the movement.

7. Telangana Students Front (TSF) – October 1998

Foundation

Founded by Khasim at Osmania University, operating under the People’s War Party ideology and affiliated with Telangana Jana Sabha.

Activities

  • Collaborated with Telangana Jana Sabha to oppose Sunnam Konda Reddy’s atrocities in Palamuru, providing ₹4 lakh compensation to affected families.
  • Launched Praja Chaitanya Yatra (2000) – a village-to-village campaign to raise awareness about Telangana’s exploitation and strengthen grassroots support.

1. Establishment of TRS (27 April 2001)

K. Chandrashekar Rao (KCR) founded the Telangana Rashtra Samithi (TRS) at Jaladrushya, the residence of Konda Lakshman Bapuji in Hyderabad. The party’s primary goal was to achieve statehood for Telangana. Prof. Jayashankar, a key intellectual figure, provided ideological support to steer the movement in the right direction.

TRS filled the political void in the Telangana movement, uniting dissatisfied and frustrated people under a common cause. The party advocated for statehood through democratic means, avoiding violence, and reinforcing the historical inevitability of a separate state.

2. KCR’s Political Journey Before TRS

  • Early Career – Began as a Youth Congress leader; joined Telugu Desam Party (TDP) in 1983, founded by N.T. Rama Rao (NTR).
  • Served as MLA from Siddipet for four consecutive terms (1985, 1989, 1994, 1999).
  • Held key positions in TDP – Transport Minister (1996–1999); Deputy Speaker after the 1999 elections.
  • Opposed Chandrababu Naidu’s “Vision 2020” document.
  • Resisted electricity tariff hikes in 2000.
  • Released a public letter highlighting Telangana’s underdevelopment.

3. Call to Action and Formation

  • Santhapuri Raghuveer Rao encouraged KCR in 2000.
  • Prof. Jayashankar predicted Telangana under KCR’s leadership.
  • KCR resigned from TDP, Deputy Speaker post, and MLA seat.
  • Established TRS on 27 April 2001.
  • Pink flag featuring Telangana map adopted.
  • 19 lakh members joined within nine days.

4. TRS Public Mobilisation and Early Success

  • Simhagarjana (17 May 2001) – First major meeting in Karimnagar.
  • Panchayat Elections (2001) – Won 1,000 MPTC, 87 ZPTC, 84 MPP seats.
  • Zilla Parishad chairmanships – Karimnagar and Nizamabad.
  • Siddipet By-election – KCR won with 58,712 majority.

5. Padayatras and National Level Mobilisation (2002–2003)

  • Padayatra – Alampur to Gadwal (180 km); meeting on 30 July.
  • Nagarjuna Sagar campaign (25 August 2003) – Kodad to Haliya.
  • Meetings – Kolhapur and Nagar Kurnool (Aug–Sep 2003).
  • Delhi Meeting (9 September 2003) – KCR elected national convener.
  • Osmania University Meeting (17 September 2003).

6. Alliance with Congress and the 2004 Elections

6.1 Background of the Alliance

The Congress party, observing TRS’s growing strength, sought an alliance to divert TDP votes and regain power. A.P. Congress Committee president D. Srinivas and A.P. in-charge Gulam Nabi Azad initiated talks. Later, Pranab Mukherji and KCR discussed the alliance in Delhi.

Congress leaders assured KCR that they would take necessary steps for Telangana’s formation after coming to power. KCR, believing the NDA was unlikely to return to power, accepted the alliance.

6.2 Electoral Agreement (25 February 2004)

  • Assembly seats – TRS would contest 42 seats in Telangana.
  • Lok Sabha seats – TRS would contest 6 out of 17 seats.
  • TRS demanded acceptance of first SRC recommendations.
  • Congress proposed formation of second SRC.
  • Congress formed Telangana Regional Congress Committee.
  • Telangana issue included in Congress manifesto.

6.3 Campaign Statements

  • Sonia Gandhi (12 March 2004, Karimnagar) – “We respect Telangana emotions.”
  • L.K. Advani – NDA would consider Telangana if Assembly consensus reached.
  • Chandrababu Naidu – Criticised KCR and opposed division.
  • Venkaiah Naidu – Criticised Congress stand on Telangana.
  • Y.S. Rajasekhara Reddy – Raised Hyderabad status issue.
  • KCR (6 May 2004) – Declared Hyderabad must be Telangana capital.

6.4 Electoral Fronts

  • Front 1 – Congress + TRS + CPI + CPM
  • Front 2 – TDP + BJP

The 2004 elections marked a turning point, bringing the Telangana issue to the centre stage of state and national politics.

1. UPA Government Formation and Common Minimum Programme

After the 2004 elections, the United Progressive Alliance (UPA) – comprising Congress, RJD, DMK, NCP, PMK, TRS, JMM, LJP, MDMK, AIMIM, PDP, IUML, RPI(A), RPI(G), and KC(M) – formed the government, with the Left parties supporting from outside. A Common Minimum Programme (CMP) was jointly designed.

Under the head Union and States Relations – Regional Development, the CMP included the Telangana issue:

“The UPA government will consider the demand for the formation of Telangana state at an appropriate time after due consultations and consensus.”

KCR had made the inclusion of this commitment a precondition for TRS’s support. The Left parties also backed the UPA, leading to the formation of the Manmohan Singh government.

2. TRS Joins the Union Government and the Ministry Issue

  • May 2004 – The UPA government allotted the Shipping Ministry to KCR and Rural Development to Ale Narendra.
  • DMK’s demand – DMK demanded the Shipping Ministry.
  • KCR’s gesture – KCR voluntarily relinquished the Shipping Ministry within 24 hours.
  • KCR served as minister without portfolio for six months.
  • Later he was given Labour and Employment Ministry.

Meanwhile, elections to the Rajya Sabha were announced. TRS had 26 MLAs and was entitled to one seat. The CPM also demanded the seat.

To help Congress, KCR gave up his Rajya Sabha membership along with his ministership.

3. President’s Address on Telangana

In the joint session of Parliament, President A.P.J. Abdul Kalam, in his address (prepared by the ruling UPA), stated:

“The government will consider the demand for the formation of Telangana state at an appropriate time after due consultations.”

Political analysts questioned the use of “consultations” and “consensus”, noting that other states had been formed without such conditions. Nonetheless, TRS succeeded in getting Telangana mentioned both in the CMP and the President’s address.

4. TRS Joins the State Government

Sonia Gandhi requested KCR to join the state government to ensure justice in the implementation of schemes. On 23 June 2004, six TRS MLAs took oath as Cabinet Ministers:

  • G. Vijaya Rama Rao – Civil Supplies
  • A. Chandra Shekar – Small Irrigation
  • T. Harish Rao – Youth Services, Printing & Stationery
  • Capt. Lakshmi Kanta Rao – Backward Classes Welfare
  • Nayani Narasimha Reddy – Technical Education, ITI
  • N. Santosh Reddy – Transport

This marked the first coalition government in Andhra Pradesh.

5. Pranab Mukherjee Committee and Consultations

Despite the CMP commitment, no concrete progress was made on Telangana. To pressure the Centre, TRS organised a large public meeting at Parade Grounds, Hyderabad, on 1 December 2004, attended by Ajit Singh, Ramdas, and Dr. Krishnan.

On 8 January 2005, the Union government appointed a cabinet sub-committee under Pranab Mukherjee (with Raghuvamsha Prasad of RJD and Dayanidhi Maran of DMK) to obtain the views of political parties on Telangana. The committee was given eight weeks to submit its report.

Responses from UPA Allies

Out of 13 UPA parties, 11 (RJD, NCP, PMK, JMM, Lok Janashakti, MDMK, TRS, etc.) wrote in favour of Telangana. DMK did not give a written letter but accepted formation in a UPA meeting on 24 August 2006. Congress did not submit a formal letter as it was handling the issue.

Outside Supporters (Left etc.)

Six parties (BSP, Rashtriya Lok Dal, Forward Bloc, Janata Dal (S), Sikkim Democratic Front, SJP(R)) supported Telangana. Samajwadi Party gave no opinion. CPM opposed formation. CPI suggested special packages and regional boards, failing which a committee should examine statehood.

Opposition (NDA)

Out of 14 NDA parties, 8 (BJP, Shiromani Akali Dal, JDU, IFD, MNF, NPF, BNS) supported Telangana. Shiv Sena, AJP, and National Conference said they would follow BJP’s decision. TDP later supported Telangana on 18 October 2008.

Although about 80% of Lok Sabha members supported Telangana, Congress did not act. KCR warned that if no decision was taken, TRS ministers would resign. The Pranab Mukherjee Committee report was not submitted even after 20 months and was never submitted thereafter.

6. TRS Ministers Resign

On the state front, Y.S. Rajasekhara Reddy consolidated his position and undertook projects like Pulichintala, Polavaram, and Singoor canal without cabinet discussions, alarming TRS. Growing restless, TRS decided to withdraw from the government.

  • 4 July 2005 – All TRS ministers except N. Santosh Reddy submitted their resignations.
  • 17 July 2005 – A large public meeting was held in Warangal to explain the reasons for withdrawal.
  • Central Minister Sharad Pawar attended and expressed support for Telangana.

7. Aftermath: Training Programmes and Continued Agitation

Telangana Jana Jagarana Sena

TRS decided to train one lakh party workers. In the first phase, 605 Preraks (motivators) were trained at Noma Function Hall, Nacharam, Hyderabad, from 10 September 2005. They received uniforms, sticks, ties, and training in ideology and physical fitness. District-level classes followed.

Polavaram Garjana (12 February 2006)

TRS organised a large meeting in Bhadrachalam in solidarity with tribals opposing the Polavaram project. Central Minister Shibu Soren attended.

Defections and Disqualifications

With YSR’s support, nine TRS MLAs signed the nomination of independent candidate Kasani Gyaneshwar in the council elections, defying the TRS whip. KCR appealed to the Speaker to disqualify them.

The Speaker issued disqualification orders two years later, on 22 December 2008.

Hyderabad Free Zone Controversy

1. Supreme Court Judgment (9 October 2009)

  • The Supreme Court, in a division bench decision, declared Hyderabad as a “Free Zone”.
  • The State Government had appointed a lawyer from Gujarat who did not adequately argue the case.
  • The Court accepted the petitioners’ argument that appointments under the Hyderabad City Police Act (1348 F) were exempt under Para 14 of the Presidential Orders.
  • The AP High Court had earlier ruled that Hyderabad should be part of the Sixth Zone.
  • The Supreme Court overturned this, allowing non-locals to continue dominating state-level jobs in Hyderabad.

2. Impact and Response

  • Seemandhra elites welcomed the decision as it protected their control over education, employment, business, and resources in Hyderabad.
  • Telangana Employees Associations demanded that Hyderabad be made part of the Sixth Zone.
  • They also demanded constitutional amendment to reverse the Supreme Court ruling.
  • The government called an all-party meeting.
  • Most parties (except TRS and Lok Satta) agreed to file a review petition.
  • Review petition had limited chances without new grounds.
  • Constitutional amendment was considered the only lasting solution.

Indian National Congress (INC): Role in Telangana Formation

The Indian National Congress played the most critical and decisive role in the formal creation of Telangana, though its journey was marked by internal contradictions, delays, and strategic shifts.

1.1 Historical Context (1956–2000)

  • 1956: Under a Congress-led government, Andhra Pradesh was formed by merging Telangana with Andhra, creating regional discontent.
  • 1969 & 1972 Movements: The Congress under Indira Gandhi absorbed the Telangana Praja Samithi and introduced the Six-Point Formula (1973) to address regional disparities, temporarily diluting the statehood demand.
  • 1992: The "Telangana Forum" was formed within the party under Jana Reddy, though it later lost momentum.
  • 2000: The "Telangana Congress Legislative Forum" was established with 41 MLAs under Chinna Reddy, successfully petitioning Sonia Gandhi and leading to the formation of a subcommittee under Dr. Manmohan Singh.

1.2 Electoral Promises and Alliance (2004)

  • March 2004: At a public meeting in Karimnagar, Sonia Gandhi promised a separate Telangana state if Congress came to power.
  • 2004 Elections: Congress formed an alliance with the TRS and included Telangana statehood in its manifesto.
  • UPA Government: After coming to power at the Centre and in the state, the Telangana issue was included in the UPA’s Common Minimum Program. A subcommittee headed by Pranab Mukherjee was formed to build consensus.

1.3 2009 Elections and Internal Divisions

  • 2009 Campaign: Congress again promised Telangana statehood. However, Y.S. Rajasekhara Reddy publicly opposed bifurcation, reflecting deep internal divisions.
  • Post-YSR Death: After YSR’s death in a helicopter crash (September 2009), K. Rosaiah became Chief Minister. The agitation intensified with KCR’s hunger strike (starting November 29, 2009).

1.4 Critical Phase: December 2009 – Reversal and Backlash

  • December 7, 2009: CM Rosaiah convened an all-party meeting. Most parties supported Telangana; Congress stated its final decision rested with the AICC.
  • December 9, 2009: Union Home Minister P. Chidambaram announced that “the process of forming Telangana will be initiated.” This led to massive protests in Seemandhra.
  • December 23, 2009: Under pressure from Seemandhra leaders, the Centre reversed course, stating Telangana formation would proceed only after a resolution in the state assembly. This reversal sparked renewed protests in Telangana.

1.5 Formation of TJAC and Congress Withdrawal (2010)

  • December 24, 2009: The Telangana Political Joint Action Committee (TJAC) was formed, including all parties and public organizations. Congress initially joined.
  • February 19, 2010: Congress withdrew from TJAC, signaling a cautious approach amid growing pressure from both regions.
  • Sri Krishna Committee (February 2010): The UPA appointed the Justice Sri Krishna Committee to explore solutions, further delaying the process.

1.6 Leadership Changes and Stalemate (2010–2013)

  • November 2010: Nallari Kiran Kumar Reddy was appointed Chief Minister, replacing Rosaiah.
  • 2011–2012: Key Congress positions (CM, PCC President, Speaker) were held by leaders from the Andhra region. Protests intensified.
  • Internal Dynamics: Despite Sonia Gandhi’s consistent support, the party feared losing parliamentary majority if Seemandhra MPs resigned, causing delays.

1.7 Final Decision and Passage (2013–2014)

  • July 30, 2013: The Congress Working Committee approved creation of Telangana with 10 districts.
  • February 2014: State Reorganization Bill passed in Lok Sabha (Feb 18) and Rajya Sabha (Feb 20).
  • March 4, 2014: June 2 declared Telangana Formation Day.
  • June 2, 2014: Telangana officially formed.

1.8 Key Takeaways on Congress Role

  • Decisive yet hesitant: Congress provided the legislative framework but faced criticism for delays.
  • Internal contradictions: Supported Telangana nationally while balancing anti-bifurcation voices.
  • Legacy: Sonia Gandhi’s backing positioned Congress as primary architect of Telangana statehood.

1. Overview

From 1952 to 2014, numerous individuals sacrificed their lives in the pursuit of establishing Telangana State. During the 1969 Telangana movement, approximately 369 students and others died. Telangana Thalli Statue – Symbol of Identity

The Telangana Thalli (Mother Telangana) statue represents the existence, culture, and self respect of Telangana.

1.1 Origin of the Concept

  • April 1975: Chief Minister Jalagam Vengala Rao organized the World Telugu Convention and promoted the concept of “Telugu Thalli”.
  • Sankarambadi Sundara Chary (Andhra poet) wrote the song “Maa Telugu Thalli Ki Mallepudanda”. Andhra rulers declared it the state anthem and mandated it in schools. The song only mentions Rani Rudramadevi, with no reference to Telangana.
  • During the same period, Telangana poet Dasarathi Krishnama Charya introduced the concept of “Telangana Thalli” through his lines:

“Koti Velugula Bangaru Konda Kinda, Parachukonnatti sarassu lopala vikasinchi, Poddu podduna andala poolu puyu, Na Telangana Kanjatavalli.”

(“Beneath the golden hill of a million lights, lodged in the lake and in the blooming beautiful flowers is my Telangana Thalli Kanjatavalli.”)

1.2 Design Evolution

Key Persons in the Initial Design

  • S. Ramulu
  • V.R. Chary (B. Venkata Ramana Chary)
  • Gangadhar
  • Narasimhulu

First Digital Form

  • B.V.R. Chary (architect of Nirmal) created a computer based image of Telangana Thalli based on the thoughts and instructions of B.S. Ramulu.
  • This early version depicted a simple woman without a crown or ornaments.

Publication & KCR’s Intervention

  • The image was published as the cover page of Prajatantra, a Telangana weekly magazine run by Devulapalli Ajay.
  • Before Namaste Telangana newspaper, Prajatantra was the only daily and weekly covering the Telangana movement.
  • KCR, after seeing the image, remarked that Telangana Thalli should not appear as a poverty stricken woman and proposed changes.

Final Design by Prof. Gangadhar

  • KCR held meetings with B.S. Ramulu, Prof. Gangadhar, Ele Laxman, Ekka Yadagiri, and others.
  • Based on collective suggestions, Prof. Gangadhar designed the present form of Telangana Thalli.
  • The first statue of this new design was made by sculptor Narasimhulu.

1.3 Unveiling of the Statue

  • First unveiling: The statue made by Narasimhulu was unveiled by Kavitha (President, Telangana Jagruthi) in front of the bus stand at Godavarikhani, after KCR had already unveiled the Telangana Thalli statue at Telangana Bhavan in 2007.
  • November 15, 2007: For the first time, the Telangana Thalli statue was unveiled by KCR at Telangana Bhavan. This statue was made by Pasunuri Dayakar.

3. Telangana Dhoom Dham – A Cultural Movement

3.1 Background & Inspiration

  • Initiated in 2002 by Rasamayi Balkishan and Anthadupula Nagaraj.
  • Inspiration came from a Jana Pada Jathara (folk festival) in Rampur organized by Anthadupula Nagaraju, which featured folk performances like Bonalu, Bathukamma, Perini, Haridasulu, and snake charmers.
  • Artists trained by Nagaraju began performing across Telangana, starting from Manda Marri (Adilabad), bringing rural traditions to urban spaces.

3.2 Conceptualization into an Organized Platform

  • Rasamayi Balkishan invited Nagaraju’s troupe to perform at the launch of his song cassette “Nelamma”. KCR, the chief guest, appreciated the folk performance.
  • A discussion between KCR, Rasamayi, and Nagaraju led to the idea of using this cultural platform to promote Telangana’s ideology, giving birth to Telangana Dhoom Dham.
  • Rasamayi integrated movement songs, singers, and poets into the cultural shows, turning Dhoom Dham into a tool for both cultural revival and political mobilization.

3.3 Milestones

  • First Show: September 30, 2002, in Kamareddy; over 50,000 attended.
  • Major Event at Lalitha Kala Thoranam, Hyderabad: Political leaders sat among the audience rather than on stage, emphasizing grassroots connection.
  • October 14, 2006: A significant Dhoom Dham event in Karimnagar during KCR’s MP by election campaign. It featured:
    • People’s singers: Gaddar, Gore Venkanna, Deshpathi Srinivas, Andesree, Midde Ramulu
    • Intellectuals: Prof. Jaya Shankar
    • Political leaders: Harish Rao, Eetela Rajender

3.4 Impact

Artists, poets, and performers like Guda Anjaiah, Andesree, Rasamayi Balkishan, and Warangal Srinivas inspired people through their cultural expressions. Telangana Dhoom Dham played a critical role in mobilizing public support for the Telangana cause.

2. Constitutional Framework – Article 3

  • Article 3 of the Indian Constitution grants Parliament the authority to create, merge, or reorganize states.
  • Dr. B.R. Ambedkar included this provision to allow a parliamentary approach for regional reorganization.
  • This prevents regional majorities within state assemblies from overpowering minority interests.

Article 3 empowers Parliament to:

Provision Description
Formation of new states By separating territory from existing states or merging areas.
Alteration of boundaries Modify boundaries to include or exclude specific territories.
Expansion or reduction of area Increase or decrease a state’s geographical area.
Renaming of states Change official names of states.

3. Step by Step Process for State Reorganization

  • Bill Preparation – The Home Ministry drafts the reorganization bill.
  • Presidential Recommendation – The bill is submitted to the President, who may seek the opinion of the concerned state assembly.
  • Parliamentary Approval – After receiving the assembly’s input, the bill is introduced in both Houses.

Note: The state assembly’s opinion is advisory; its approval or rejection does not bind the President or Parliament.

4. Assembly’s Role and the Telangana Bill

  • When the Telangana Bill was presented in the Andhra Pradesh Assembly:
  • 175 Seemandhra MLAs opposed it.
  • 119 Telangana MLAs supported it.
  • The discussion allowed Andhra Pradesh to request grants, concessions, and additional funding.
  • Seemandhra MLAs, by rejecting the bill, missed the chance to negotiate financial packages.

5. Bill’s Journey to Parliament

  • After reviewing inputs, the President forwarded the bill to the Home Department with modifications.
  • The bill was approved by the Central Cabinet.
  • Introduced in both Houses of Parliament.
  • Constitutional Requirement: Simple majority required.
  • Quorum: 10% of members required for discussion.
  • Most Andhra representatives opposed the discussion, except M. Venkaiah Naidu.

6. UPA Government’s Handling of Telangana (2004–2014)

6.1. 2004 – Formation of UPA and Inclusion of Telangana in CMP

  • Indian National Congress emerged as the largest party in the 2004 General Elections.
  • UPA formed with Dr. Manmohan Singh as PM and Sonia Gandhi as Chairperson.
  • TRS joins UPA Cabinet: KCR agreed to join on condition that separate Telangana be included in the Common Minimum Programme (CMP).
  • Sonia Gandhi, in consultation with Lalu Prasad Yadav (RJD) and Sharad Pawar (NCP), included Telangana’s formation in the CMP, stating it would be pursued with consultation from all political parties.

6.2. Pranab Mukherjee Committee

  • A committee led by Pranab Mukherjee (with Raghuvansh Prasad Singh and Dayanidhi Maran) was formed to review the Telangana issue.
  • Political parties were asked for their stance; 36 of 38 parties supported formation.
  • Despite overwhelming support, the committee did not submit a final report – reportedly due to pressure from Andhra Pradesh’s then Chief Minister Y.S. Rajasekhara Reddy, who threatened the resignation of 20 Andhra MPs, which could have destabilized the UPA government.

6.3. 2009 – Momentum and Key Events

  • September 2, 2009: Y.S. Rajasekhara Reddy died in a helicopter crash.
  • November 30, 2009: KCR began a hunger strike, leading to massive protests across Telangana as his health worsened.
  • Emergency Meeting: Home Minister P. Chidambaram announced the initiation of the process for Telangana’s formation – widely seen as a “birthday gift” from Sonia Gandhi to Telangana. Satisfied, KCR ended his fast.
  • Seemandhra Resignations: The announcement triggered mass resignations of Seemandhra MPs, fearing state division.
  • Chidambaram’s Reversal: Facing political fallout, Chidambaram announced that wider consultations would be conducted before proceeding, leading to discontent and a wave of suicides among Telangana’s youth and students.

6.4. Final Decision and Formation (2014)

  • Despite delays, the UPA government decided to proceed with Telangana’s formation just before the 2014 elections.
  • Telangana officially became the 29th state of India on June 2, 2014.

13. Sri Krishna Committee

1. Formation

  • Established: February 3, 2010 by the Central Government
  • Chairperson: Justice Sri Krishna
  • Purpose: Examine the Telangana issue and explore possible solutions.
  • Methodology: Traveled across Telangana, gathered nearly one lakh memorandums, consulted various sections.
  • Report Submitted: December 30, 2010 (505 page report in two volumes to the Home Ministry)

2. Six Proposed Solutions

  • Keep Andhra Pradesh united.
  • Divide the state; make Hyderabad a Union Territory with separate capitals for Andhra and Telangana.
  • Form “Rayala Telangana” – merge Rayalaseema with Telangana; Coastal Andhra a separate state; Hyderabad part of Rayala Telangana.
  • Divide into Seemandhra and Telangana; Hyderabad a Union Territory with expanded boundaries.
  • Divide into Seemandhra and Telangana; Hyderabad capital of Telangana; Seemandhra gets a new capital.
  • Keep state united but establish a Statutory Telangana Regional Council with socio economic measures.

3. Controversy

  • The report had nine chapters; eight were sealed.
  • Pandit Narayana Reddy (former MP from Nizamabad) filed a PIL against the committee in the High Court.
  • High Court Ruling: The Sri Krishna Committee had exceeded its limits.

4. Conclusion of the Process

Despite delays and the formation of multiple committees, the UPA government ultimately proceeded with the bifurcation. The Andhra Pradesh Reorganisation Act, 2014 was passed, and Telangana was officially formed on June 2, 2014, as the 29th state of India.

14. Political and Media Management During the Telangana Movement

1. Political Management

  • Challenge for Congress: The ruling party aimed for unity among its leaders to counter TRS influence.
  • Proposed Strategies:
    • Appoint a leader from Telangana as Chief Minister or Deputy Chief Minister to potentially reduce momentum for the movement.
    • Clarify the party’s stance on Telangana and discourage members from supporting the movement.
    • Form a ministerial committee to continue discussions, signaling readiness to address the committee’s report.
  • Context: TRS had announced plans for non cooperation and hinted at civil conflict, gaining support from activist Gaddar.

2. Media Management

  • Media Landscape: Telangana had 13 electronic channels and 5 regional newspapers. Most were Seemandhra owned, with Seemandhra editors and staff, aligning with the united Andhra narrative.
  • Sympathetic Reporters: Despite ownership, Telangana reporters in Hyderabad were sympathetic to the cause, fueling local support.
  • Strategies:
    • Replace Telangana reporters with Seemandhra ones in key positions to shift public sentiment.
    • Use government advertising dependency to influence content, focusing headlines to minimize pro Telangana coverage.

3. Security Preparedness

  • Deployment: Additional police forces equipped with low intensity weaponry were positioned, especially near Osmania and Kakatiya universities.
  • Intelligence: Units were placed on high alert to monitor developments and ensure rapid response.
  • Anticipation: Significant protests were expected for a few months post announcement; preparations were made to counter misinformation that might incite emotions.

    • 87 members spoke; 9,072 amendments proposed.

3. Assurances by Prime Minister Manmohan Singh

During Rajya Sabha discussions, the Prime Minister provided six assurances to Andhra Pradesh:

  • Special Development Package for Rayalaseema and North Coastal Andhra (similar to KBK in Odisha and Bundelkhand in MP).
  • Incentives for Industrialization and Economic Growth in both states.
  • Special Status for Andhra Pradesh for five years, covering four Rayalaseema and three North Coastal districts.
  • Polavaram Project to be executed by the Central Government, including rehabilitation and reconstruction.
  • Deficit Financing for Andhra Pradesh for 2014–15 through central budgetary support.
  • Resolution of Staff, Finance, Assets, and Liabilities distribution issues.
